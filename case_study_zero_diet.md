# Case Study: “Zero” vs “Diet” Beverages — Health Claims, Branding, and Consumer Choice

**Prepared for:** Interested stakeholders in food & beverage marketing, public health professionals, and consumer advocates

**Date:** February 17, 2026

---

## Executive summary

This case study examines whether "Zero" and "Diet" labeled beverages meaningfully promote healthier consumer choices and awareness, or whether they primarily function as strategic branding tools designed to retain customer loyalty amid health controversies. Drawing on marketing theory, consumer psychology, public-health evidence, and real-world brand behavior, the study concludes that both forces operate simultaneously: these products offer lower-calorie alternatives that can help some consumers reduce sugar intake, but their naming, packaging, and portfolio-placement are often optimized to minimize brand flight and preserve purchase frequency rather than to advance public health.

Key findings:

* **Nutritional benefit is real but limited.** Replacing sugar-sweetened beverages (SSBs) with zero-calorie sweetened versions reduces caloric intake, but evidence on long-term weight loss or cardiometabolic risk reduction is mixed and depends on substitution patterns.
* **Branding outweighs education.** The terms "Zero" and "Diet" are used to signal healthier options while maintaining core brand identity; marketing spend, product placement, and flavor mimicry prioritize retention over consumer education about overall diet.
* **Regulatory & labeling gaps matter.** Inconsistent labeling, lack of front-of-pack clarity, and loose regulation regarding health claims enable positioning that can mislead consumers about healthfulness.
* **Behavioral risk — the health halo and compensation.** Consumers may overconsume or compensate (eating more calories elsewhere) when consuming low-calorie beverages, reducing net benefit.

Recommendations for policy-makers and public-health groups include stronger front-of-pack labeling, consumer education campaigns about overall diet context, and closer monitoring of marketing to vulnerable groups (children, adolescents).

## 1. Background and market context

Over the last three decades beverage manufacturers launched large portfolios of sugar-reduced alternatives, commonly branded with labels such as "Diet," "Light," and more recently "Zero." These products arose in response to rising consumer health consciousness, scientific attention to sugar-related harms, and regulatory pressure (e.g., sugar taxes, public-health campaigns). The commercial objective: preserve popular flavor profiles and brand equity while offering a lower-calorie option that fits declining sugar tolerance among some consumer segments.

Market dynamics driving the phenomena include:

* Consumer demand for calorie control and weight management products.
* Rising awareness of links between excess sugar and metabolic disease.
* Competitive necessity: brands that lack a diet/zero variant risk losing customers to rivals who offer a similar-tasting low-calorie product.
* Retailer slotting and promotional economics favor portfolio breadth.

---

## 2. Nutritional science: what does "zero" actually deliver?

**Calorie reduction.** Switching from full-sugar SSBs to zero‑calorie sweetened beverages (ZCSBs) removes the sugar-derived calories in the drink itself. For an adult who replaces one 330–355 mL sugary drink per day, this can reduce daily caloric intake by ~120–150 kcal.

**Metabolic and weight outcomes.** Randomized controlled trials (short-term) show that replacing sugar-sweetened beverages with non-caloric alternatives reduces weight gain versus continuing sugar intake. However, observational studies produce mixed signals: associations between artificial sweetener consumption and weight gain, diabetes, or cardiometabolic risk may reflect reverse causation or confounding (people at higher cardiometabolic risk choose diet drinks).

**Gut microbiome & sweet taste signaling.** Emerging research explores whether non-nutritive sweeteners alter gut microbiota or metabolic signaling; evidence is still evolving and does not yet provide definitive grounds to ban these products.

**Net public-health impact depends on substitution.** Benefits occur when ZCSBs replace caloric drinks. If they are added on top of existing calorie intake or cause compensatory eating, net improvements will be modest or null.

## 3. Branding strategy and marketing mechanics

Brand teams design "Zero" and "Diet" options to achieve several commercial aims:

* **Retention of flavor/brand cues.** Packaging, typography, and flavor names closely mirror flagship products so consumers continue to associate the product with the same taste promise.
* **Portfolio protection.** Introduce low-calorie variants rather than changing the flagship recipe (which risks alienating loyal consumers).
* **Health signaling without medical claims.** Use clean, modern cues (black or minimalist labels for "Zero") to imply modern, healthier alternatives while avoiding regulated health claims.
* **Price and promotional parity.** Keep promotional schedules and shelf placement similar so habitual buyers can switch with minimal friction.

These tactics suggest marketing priority is to reduce churn and capture health-conscious segments while maintaining existing buyer habits.

---

## 4. Consumer perception and behavioral response

**Health halo.** A classic phenomenon where a product labeled as "diet" or "zero" is perceived as broadly healthier—consumers may infer permission to consume more or reduce other healthful behaviors.

**Taste and satisfaction.** Brands invest in flavor technology to mimic sugar; perceived taste parity increases substitution but also sustains cravings.

**Demographic patterns.** Younger consumers and urban professionals adopt zero/diet options more quickly. Gender differences are notable in some markets (women historically higher users of "diet" beverages in certain countries), though these patterns are shifting.

**Information asymmetry.** Many consumers misunderstand the role of zero/diet drinks in an overall healthy diet and may treat them as weight-loss tools rather than sugar-replacement strategies.

---

## 5. Regulation, labeling, and ethical considerations

Regulatory frameworks vary by country. Key issues:

* **Front-of-pack claims.** Lack of uniform front-of-pack labeling allows brands to avoid clear messaging (e.g., "contains artificial sweeteners").
* **Child-directed marketing.** Concerns arise when diet/zero marketing targets adolescents — a vulnerable group for lifelong taste preferences.
* **Health claims vs. permitted statements.** Brands must avoid explicit health claims ("reduces obesity risk") but can use non-specific claims that imply health benefits.

Policy remedies include mandatory disclosure of added sweeteners, clearer front-of-pack nutrient icons, and marketing restrictions for children.

## 7. Evidence synthesis: health promotion or loyalty preservation?

Weighing the evidence:

* **Health-promoting aspects:** Clear per-unit calorie reduction when substitution occurs; potential to lower population sugar intake if substitution is widespread.
* **Branding/loyalty aspects:** Naming conventions, packaging, and marketing tactics strongly favor retention of the parent brand’s consumer base and regular purchase frequency.

Conclusion: Zero/diet beverages are a hybrid phenomenon. They carry genuine potential to reduce sugar intake at scale but are simultaneously constructed and promoted in ways that prioritize brand continuity and revenue protection. That dual nature moderates their public-health promise.

## 8. Recommendations

**For policymakers & public-health advocates**

1. Strengthen front-of-pack disclosure of non‑nutritive sweeteners and sugar content.
2. Monitor and regulate child-directed marketing of sweetened and zero-sweetened beverages.
3. Promote educational campaigns emphasizing whole-diet changes rather than single-product swaps.

**For brands that genuinely want to promote public health**

1. Adopt clearer labeling and voluntary reformulation targets across portfolios (not just new variants).
2. Limit marketing that implies broad health benefits; support public-health literacy.
3. Prioritize portion-control packaging and transparent communication about sweeteners.

**For consumers**

1. Use zero/diet beverages as a strategy to reduce sugar only if they replace, not supplement, sugary drinks.
2. Pay attention to overall calorie intake and dietary patterns rather than relying on a single product for weight control.
