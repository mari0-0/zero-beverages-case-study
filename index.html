Research Questions

What We Investigated

Five critical research questions guiding this investigation ‚Äî examining perception, marketing, health literacy, sensory experience, and consumer behavior.

How do consumers interpret and differentiate health messages associated with "Zero" versus "Diet" beverage labels? This question focuses on perceived health meaning and semantic framing ‚Äî how the language itself shapes consumer expectations.

What effects do different marketing narratives ‚Äî health benefits, taste authenticity, lifestyle positioning ‚Äî have on consumers' perception of these products as health-oriented? This assesses marketing influence on cognition and belief.

How does consumer health awareness influence trust in health claims made by "Zero" and "Diet" beverages? This explores the moderating role of health literacy ‚Äî whether more informed consumers are less susceptible to branding cues.

In what ways does sensory similarity to original sugary drinks affect consumers' behavioral loyalty to the brand? This connects product experience with psychological attachment ‚Äî whether taste parity drives repeat purchase.

Do consumers of "Zero" and "Diet" beverages exhibit different patterns of category retention ‚Äî continuing soft drink consumption compared to switching to non-carbonated or naturally healthier alternatives? This links branding outcomes with actual consumption behavior.

Conclusion

A Balanced Evaluation

The Dual Nature of Zero & Diet Beverages

This case study reveals that zero and diet beverages operate at the intersection of genuine nutritional utility and sophisticated brand management. Neither a pure health product nor a cynical marketing ploy, they exist in a complex middle ground.

Health-Promoting Dimensions

When zero-calorie beverages replace sugar-sweetened drinks, they deliver clear per-unit calorie reduction. If substitution occurs at scale, population sugar intake could decline meaningfully ‚Äî a real public-health contribution.

Branding & Loyalty Dimensions

Naming conventions, packaging design, and marketing tactics are engineered to retain the parent brand's consumer base and maintain regular purchase frequency. The commercial architecture prioritizes revenue continuity over dietary education.

The Verdict

The dual nature of these products moderates their public-health promise. Consumers should view them as one tool within a broader dietary strategy ‚Äî not a health solution in themselves. The industry, meanwhile, must be held accountable for marketing that implies broader health benefits than the evidence supports.

Implications for Consumers

1 Use zero/diet beverages as a strategy to reduce sugar only if they replace ‚Äî not supplement ‚Äî sugary drinks
2 Focus on overall calorie intake and dietary patterns rather than relying on a single product
3 Be aware of the health halo effect and compensatory eating behaviors

Implications for Brands

1 Adopt clearer labeling and voluntary reformulation targets across entire portfolios
2 Limit marketing that implies broad health benefits beyond calorie reduction
3 Support public-health literacy and transparent sweetener communication
